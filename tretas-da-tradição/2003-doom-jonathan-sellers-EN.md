# Notes from the archivist & introduction to this mess

### Who is Jonathan Sellers?

Besides being one of my favourite figures in online occult culture, Jonathan Sellers was the founder of _Antiquities of the Illuminati_, an online research group, mailing list, and website dedicated to the history of Western Esotericism, Freemasonry, Rosicrucianism, and Aleister Crowley’s philosophy of Thelema.

In the late 90s and early 2000s, Sellers was a prolific content creator and archivist in these circles. He took himself and his work very seriously-as evidenced by the trademark symbol next to Antiquities of the Illuminati™ and his disdain for "cheap Angelfire pages." Same.

### Timeline of the mess

- **The September 2001 Incident**  
Sellers mentions an "offending personality" who disrupted his mailing list around September 2001. In the highly charged post-9/11 atmosphere, online forums were rife with flame wars. I wish I wasn't literally a toddler so I could have seen it with my very own eyes... Anyway, this unnamed individual allegedly caused chaos on Sellers’ list, and when Sellers tried to moderate them, the person pushed back, calling Sellers a "jaded hippy" kek

- **The Summer 2002 "Censure"**    
Disgusted by this behavior, Sellers teamed up with a group of other prominent online occultists. In June 2002, they drafted and signed a formal "petition/censure instrument." Essentially, it was a block-and-ban lis of Ye Olde Internete, a formal declaration by a group of elders stating that this "offending personality" was a persona non grata in their community.

- **The Broken Oaths and Slur-Filled Emails**  
This is where the gossip gets juicy. After the censure, the targeted individual didn’t take it lying down. They retaliated by breaking a confidence or an "oath" of privacy between "two grown men." The offender leaked private information about Sellers’ personal life and bombarded him with incredibly hostile, toxic emails. Sellers notes he was condemned as being "unemployed, sick, with no sexual equipment, no manhood, a leech, sitting in the dark all the time, etc." Again, same.

- **The 2003 Cross-Posting Drama**  
The catalyst for this specific document happened in 2003. Someone (either the original offender or an ally) took the private details of this 2002 censure and posted them on a completely unrelated, public mailing list that had nothing to do with Thelema. Sellers was furious about the bad PR, fearing that public exposure would make the general public conflate Thelema with dangerous cults or Satanism.

In 2003, the occult world was transitioning from secretive, physical lodges to open internet forums. Figures like Sellers tried to maintain the rigid, hierarchical structure of traditional secret societies (using words like Brethren, Oaths, and Censure) on platforms like Yahoo! Groups, where they ultimately had very little actual control over information. Sellers’ rant about how "image is everything" reflects a real worry among 2000s occultists about how they were perceived by a post-Satanic Panic public. They were terrified of being lumped in with "edgelord" Satanists or being exposed to the secular world as fragile egoists. Wish they would still keep that in mind nowadays.

Jonathan Sellers, with quotations by others.

c.2003

 	
  It has recently come to our attention that certain statements have been made on certain lists, not our own, that identify us as having been party to certain activities during the summer of 2002. These statements were made in violation of an oath made by the person making said statements that charged said individual not to betray the Brethren.

  The only thing that need be stated is that we signed a petition / censure instrument, and engaged in email exchanges that were rather non-stop, for a good two weeks running. After the initial push we really did not have any interest in proceeding any further because we have better things to do with our time and, for that matter, email itself can take up too much of our time due to the responsibilities we carry out on a daily basis.

  Creating content is a job, like any other job.

  Now, there are some who will ask: "Who cares?" "What has this to do with me?" …and other nagging questions.

  Well, even in a Thelemic context, such things as morals and ethics DO exist. Is this a shock, or a contradiction in terms? Perhaps, but it is true. If a person makes a commitment to do something, then they must do what they say they are going to do. Otherwise they are a liar and a fool. Similarly, if a person derives learning from what we produce and/or compile together, then that person must pay for the teaching. These things relate to something little practiced in today's world, the Obligation, or Oath.

  In Theoretic Masonry, the Oath binds a candidate not to reveal the secrets of the degree being conferred upon said candidate.

  What would happen if politicians and businesses were forced to be honest? The world would stop.

  At any rate, we gave no consent for our name to be mentioned in connection with certain activities. Furthermore, because an Oath was broken, we deem it necessary for said individual to be reminded of said Oath taking. “Greater Thelema” and “Antiquities of the Illuminati™” are not mere reading societies, after all! Were that the case, we might just as well have a cheap Angelfire page and call it NewAeon Golden Dawn or something passé like that!

  In a system where more power is conferred on the individual, through self-work, self-realization, self-initiation, then one must come to Know One's Own Self! If the Individual is the Center of that Individual's Universe, and all others are as planets in that individual's orbit -- then that Individual must Know Itself! That being the case, then what does that say about some of these people who are in positions of imperfect self-revelation? Are we perfect ourselves? No, and Hell No! Do we expect perfection in others? No, because what good would that be? Life would be completely dreadful if everything and everybody were perfect. That is one of the problems with the worldview exhibited by people conditioned by the sin complex. To wish that everything were perfect, unlike this dirty evil sinful world is to wish for oblivion and annihilation.

  Similarly, in the Thelemic world-view, it may be stated that the Word of Sin is Restriction; yet -- it is also clear that one's will is not to be infringed upon. Some might argue that what we were party to constituted infringement of will in the case of the individual who was censured last June (2002), yet one must go back to the activities of 2001 to see why we would sign our name to the censure. When enough madness and grief was taking place, in the world and in our own personal sphere of operation, having our “Antiquities of the Illuminati” group spammed with senseless bickering and hate- filled rhetoric was completely unwarranted. We are not politically correct, nor do we advocate political correctness, because that is what the real bigots use to hide behind.

  I mean, it's my list, my group, my website, my creation, after all. I reserve the rights to refuse service and not serve refuse to anyone I see fit to. That is how it should be with all such lists and websites. So, it was perfectly naturally for me to be disgusted with such unwarranted activities on the part of the offending personality of September 2001 c.e. However, it was met with indignation of the type that suggested that I, not that particular person, was the problem and furthermore, that I was some sort of jaded hippy for being so disgusted. Well, several persons, many, in fact, as it turned out, were as disgusted with it as my associates and I were.

  This behavior cost us. Therefore we were fully within our rights to sign a censure (even if it has been suggested that the censure was more along the lines of an exercise). Now, what takes place following the signing of our name? We get an onslaught of emails condemning us, as being unemployed, sick, with no sexual equipment, no manhood, a leech, sitting in the dark all the time, etc.; indicating things which were promised by said recipient of said censure, to be held in the strictest of confidence. In short, another breaking of an oath between what we originally assumed were two grown men. Well, if we choose to tell someone about ourselves, then that is our choice. True, we have to realize that, the more public our personality becomes, the more people shall know about us. Well, we do not have all that much to hide, after all, but we still prefer privacy to transparency. The work we do is more important to us than personality considerations, even if we like to inflate our ego with our language and our art manipulations. We are not engaging in this work, so we can get in someone's pants. We enjoy being in our own pants plenty enough, thank you very much! Nobody can surpass that in our way of being; therefore why wreck the painting???

Now, if the post had something to do with Thelema, it is clear to us that it should not be circulated in a public forum having little or nothing to do with Thelema. It is not a good act of public relations and, contrary to what some may think, that matters. Because of the general low-rent tendency we have come across in this racket, continual bad press is not a good thing; and it is not a good thing in times such as we find ourselves alive in. In our way of seeing things and, that is to say in a very general sense that is in conformity with the True Will of Planet Earth, Thelema is the logical outcome of the evolution of Philosophy, of Science, of Religious Tradition and of Consciousness Expansion.   Obligation. The solemn promise made by a Mason on his admission into any degree is technically called his obligation. In a legal sense, obligation is synonymous with duty. Its derivation shows its true meaning, for the Latin word obigatio literally signifies a tying or binding. The obligation is that which binds a man to do some act, the doing of which thus becomes his duty. By his obligation, a Mason is bound or tied to his Order. Hence the Romans called the military oath which was taken by the soldier his obligation, and, too, it is said that it is the obligation that makes the Mason. Before that ceremony, there is no tie that binds the candidate to the Order so as to make him a part of it; after the ceremony, the tie has been completed, and the candidate becomes at once a Mason, entitled to all the rights and privileges and subject to all the duties and responsibilities that enure in that character. The jurists have divided obligations into imperfect and perfect, or natural and civil. In Masonry there is no such distinction. The Masonic obligation is that moral one which, although it cannot be enforced by the courts of law, is binding on the party who makes it, in conscience and according to moral justice. It varies in each degree, but in each is perfect. Its different clauses, in which different duties are prescribed, are called its points, which are either affirmative or negative, a division like that of the precepts of the Jewish law. The affirmative points are those which require certain acts to be performed; the negative points are those which forbid certain other acts to be done. The whole of them is preceded by a general point of secrecy, common to all the degrees, and this point is called the tie.

From Morals and Dogma, Chapter 28, pp. 721-724, we find the following:

  Morality has its axioms, like the other sciences; and these axioms are, in all languages, justly termed moral truths. Moral truths, considered in themselves, are equally as certain as mathematical truths. Given the idea of a deposit, the idea of keeping it faithfully is attached to it as necessarily, as to the idea of a triangle is attached the idea that its three angles are equal to two right angles. You may violate a deposit; but in doing so, do not imagine that you change the nature of things, or make what is in itself a deposit become your own property. The two ideas exclude each other. You have but a false semblance of property: and all the efforts of the passions, all the sophisms of interest, will not overturn essential differences. Therefore it is that a moral truth is so imperious; because, like all truth, it is what it is, and shapes itself to please no caprice. Always the same, and always present, little as we may like it, it inexorably condemns, with a voice always heard, but not always regarded, the insensate and guilty will which thinks to prevent its existing, by denying, or rather by pretending to deny, its existence.

  The moral truths are distinguished from other truths by this singular characteristic: so soon as we perceive them, they appear to us as the rule of our conduct. If it is true that a deposit is made in order to be returned to its legitimate possessor, it must be returned. To the necessity of believing the truth, the necessity practicing it is added.

  The necessity of practicing the moral truths is obligation. The moral truths, necessary to the eye of reason, are obligatory on the will. The moral obligation, like the moral truth which is its basis, is absolute. As necessary truths are not more or less necessary, so obligation is not more or less obligatory. There are degrees of importance among different obligations; but there are no degrees in the obligation itself. One is not nearly obliged, almost obliged; but wholly so, or not at all. If there be any place of refuge against the obligation, it ceases to exist.

  If the obligation is absolute, it is immutable and universal. For if what is obligation to-day may not be so to-morrow, if what is obligatory for me may not be so for you, the obligation differing from itself, it would be relative and contingent. This fact of absolute, immutable, universal obligation is certain and manifest. The good is the foundation of obligation. If it be not, obligation has no foundation; and that is impossible. If one act ought to be done, and another ought not, it must be because evidently there is an essential difference between the two acts. If one be not good and the other bad, the obligation imposed on us is arbitrary.

  To make the Good a consequence, of anything whatever, is to annihilate it. It is the first, or it is nothing. When we ask an honest man why, despite his urgent necessities, he has respected the sanctity of a deposit, he answers, because it was his duty. Asked why it was his duty, he answers, because it was right, was just, was good. Beyond that there is no answer to be made, but there is also no question to be asked. No one permits a duty to be imposed on him without giving himself a reason for it: but when it is admitted that the duty is commanded by justice, the mind is satisfied; for it has arrived at a principle beyond which there is nothing to seek, justice being its own principle. The primary truths include their own reason: and justice, the essential distinction between good and evil, is the first truth of morality.

  Justice is not a consequence; because we cannot ascend to any principle above it. Moral truth forces itself on man, and does not emanate from him. It no more becomes subjective, by appearing to us obligatory, than truth does by appearing to us necessary. It is in the very nature of the true and the good that we must seek for the reason of necessity and obligation. Obligation is founded on the necessary distinction between the good and the evil; and it is itself the foundation of liberty. If man has his duties to perform, he must have the faculty of accomplishing them, of resisting desire, passion, and interest, in order to obey the law. He must be free; therefore he is so, or human nature is in contradiction with itself. The certainty of the obligation involves the corresponding certainty of free will.

  It is the will that is free: though sometimes that will may be ineffectual. The power to do must not be confounded with the power to will. The former may be limited: the latter is sovereign. The external effects may be prevented: the resolution itself cannot. Of this sovereign power of the will we are conscious. We feel in ourselves, before it becomes determinate, the force which can determine itself in one way or another. At the same time when I will this or that, I am equally conscious that I can will the contrary. I am conscious that I am the master of my resolution: that I may check it, continue it, retake it. When the act has ceased, the consciousness of the power which produced it has not. That consciousness and the power remain, superior to all the manifestations of the power. Wherefore free-will is the essential and ever-subsisting attribute of the will itself.

  At the same time that we judge that a free agent has done a good or a bad act, we form another judgment, as necessary as the first; that if he has done well, he deserves compensation; if ill, punishment. That judgment may be expressed in a manner more or less vivid, according as it is mingled with sentiments more or less ardent. Sometimes it will be a merely kind feeling toward a virtuous agent, and moderately hostile to a guilty one; sometimes enthusiasm or indignation. The judgment of merit and demerit is intimately connected with the judgment of good and evil. Merit is the natural right which we have to be rewarded; demerit the natural right which others have to punish us. But whether the reward is received, or the punishment undergone, or not, the merit or demerit equally subsists. Punishment and reward are the satisfaction of merit and demerit, but do not constitute them. Take away the former, and the latter continue. Take away the latter, and there are no longer real rewards or punishments. When a base man encompasses our merited honors, he has obtained the mere appearance of a reward; a mere material advantage. The reward is essentially moral; and its value is independent of its form. One of those simple crowns of oak with which the early Romans rewarded heroism, was of more real value than the wealth of the world, when it was the sign of the gratitude and admiration of a people. Reward accorded to merit is a debt; without merit it is an alms or a theft.

  The Good is good in itself, and to be accomplished, whatever the consequences. The results of the Good cannot but be fortunate. Happiness, separated from the Good, is but a fact to which no moral idea is attached. As an effect of the Good, it enters into the moral order, completes and crowns it.
